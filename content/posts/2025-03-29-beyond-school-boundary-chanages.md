In the [previous blog post](/posts/technical-aspects-of-redrawing-school-attendance-boundaries/), I covered the technical side of our boundary-redrawing process: data organization, geospatial analysis, and the optimization algorithms we used. This post is about what the data can't capture.

School consolidation ultimately comes down to students, families, and communities. Here's what I learned about getting that part right.

## Community Engagement Starts Before You Have a Plan

School boundary changes demand real public consultation — not because regulations require it, but because families will spend years living with the results.

In practice, that means starting well before any proposal exists. The instinct is to gather feedback on a draft; the better move is to build relationships before the draft is written. That shift matters most for communities that face barriers to traditional participation: families who speak languages other than English, parents with disabilities, people who work nights and can't make a 6 p.m. town hall.

Transparency is the foundation. Share the data you're using, explain the criteria you're optimizing for, and name the tradeoffs honestly. When people understand why a boundary is drawn a certain way, resistance tends to look more like negotiation and less like opposition. Keep feedback channels open after decisions are made, too. The process doesn't end at adoption.

What I got wrong early on: I treated engagement as a phase. It's not. The relationships you build (or don't) before the process starts determine how much trust you have when things get hard.

## Equity Means More Than Balanced Enrollment Numbers

Boundary decisions shape who goes to school with whom, who has a long commute, and who gets access to advanced coursework. They can reinforce existing inequities or chip away at them. They rarely do neither.

Start with the data. Enrollment analysis and measures like the [dissimilarity index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_of_dissimilarity) tell you where segregation exists and how severe it is. But the dissimilarity index doesn't tell you that low-income students in the proposed zone average a 30-minute commute while higher-income students average 10 — that requires looking harder. Similarly, routing special education through magnet programs instead of neighborhood schools can look neutral on paper while creating a significant busing burden in practice.

Opportunity mapping fills in what enrollment counts miss: which schools have experienced teachers, which offer AP courses, which have after-school programs. When you redraw zones, you can use that analysis to direct resources toward schools that are gaining enrollment from historically underserved students.

Then monitor. Equity isn't a box you check before the boundary change goes into effect.

## Resistance Is the Default. Plan for It.

Families who've spent years attached to a particular school aren't being irrational when they push back on rezoning. That attachment is real. The fear of disruption — new commutes, different peer groups, unfamiliar cultures — is legitimate even when the proposed changes are justified.

The mistake is treating resistance as a PR problem to manage. It's actually a signal about what's at stake for people.

What actually helps: name the concerns explicitly rather than waiting for families to raise them. If a proposed change means some students will have longer commutes, say so up front and explain why the tradeoff is worth making. Phase changes when you can — giving families a year to adjust to a new assignment reduces the sense of upheaval. And invest in the receiving schools, not just the boundaries. A family is more likely to accept a new assignment if the school they're being assigned to is visibly improving.

## Legal Compliance Is the Floor, Not the Framework

The legal landscape for school boundary changes includes civil rights requirements, prohibitions on racial gerrymandering, and — in California — specific obligations for districts under conservatorship that tie consolidation to equity and community engagement standards.

Know the requirements. Consult legal counsel early, document your analysis and rationale thoroughly, and make that documentation public. If a decision is challenged, you want a clear record of why you drew the boundary where you did.

But here's the thing I'd push back on: treating legal requirements as the primary framework for how to run a consolidation process tends to produce the minimum viable effort. The legal floor is a starting point. The harder question is what an equitable process looks like beyond what's required — and that's a policy question, not a legal one.

## Property Values Are Real, But They're Not the Goal

Boundary changes affect housing markets. Families know it, real estate agents know it, and school boards know it. Pretending otherwise doesn't help anyone.

What I've seen is that property value concerns often function as a proxy for other anxieties — about school quality, about demographic change, about losing access to resources families feel entitled to. Addressing those anxieties directly is more useful than trying to reassure people about home prices.

When equity concerns and property value concerns genuinely conflict, equity has to come first. That's a hard thing to say in a public meeting, but it's the honest answer. Districts can reduce the financial sting through targeted investments in affected schools and community development efforts tied to the consolidation — but those are mitigation strategies, not reasons to draw boundaries differently.

## The Long Game

Boundary decisions don't just assign students to buildings. They shape which neighborhoods develop social cohesion around shared schools, which students build friendships across income lines, and — over time — what kind of communities these districts become.

That's an argument for taking the long view when evaluating tradeoffs. A boundary change that's politically easier in year one but concentrates disadvantage in a handful of schools will be harder to undo later. The short-term resistance to a more equitable map is real, but so is the cost of getting it wrong.

I don't have a clean framework for weighing those considerations. What I do have is more respect for how much hangs on decisions that can look, from the outside, like lines on a map.
